The problem

Most playspaces were built for children. Few have become children's places.

Research involving children with and without disabilities consistently reveals the same gaps: too little variety in play opportunities, too little challenge — especially for older children and those with diverse abilities — and features that don't genuinely invite participation from all users.

Meanwhile, professionals responsible for playground provision often lack structured methods for assessing these qualities, making it difficult to identify gaps or justify improvements.

"Not every place built for the purpose of play is a children's place. A place built for children can become a children's place when children connect to it through outdoor play — when they can attribute meaning to the environment."

Rasmussen (2004), as cited in Morgenthaler et al., 2023

Accessibility ≠ usability

Accessibility helps a child reach the equipment. Usability asks whether children with diverse abilities can actually participate, explore, challenge themselves, and belong.

Adults count; children play

Most assessments describe what a playspace contains. COPA asks a different question: what does the environment afford children to do, experience, and choose?

What COPA measures

Two constructs. Four scales.

Every item in the audit is assessed across four scales, generating scores on two primary constructs that reflect what the research identifies as the core dimensions of high-quality outdoor play environments.

Play Value

66 items

Conceptualized as the diverse, multiple, and potential play affordances supported by varied and combined environmental features. A space high in play value invites children into many different kinds of play.

"Play value of a playground is conceptualized by the diverse, multiple, and potential play affordances supported by diverse, varied and combined environmental features." — Morgenthaler et al., 2025, p. 94
Usability

71 items

Beyond accessibility. Environmental qualities that enable children with diverse abilities to play — not merely to arrive at the equipment, but to participate, belong, explore, and choose.

Builds on Iwarsson & Ståhl (2003): usability has a stronger focus on equitable use and inclusion than accessibility alone.

The four scales

Q
Provision (Quantity)

In what quantity is this feature or environmental characteristic provided? None / Some / A lot.

V
Variety

To what extent is there variety in this feature or characteristic? Are the options varied or all the same? No variety / Some / A lot.

C
Challenge Opportunities

To what extent does this feature provide different levels of challenge for individual children or different abilities? No / Some / A lot.

S
Sociability Support

Can more than one child use this feature or space together? No / Yes — a pair / Yes — more than two.

The whole playspace

Not just the equipment. Everything.

COPA evaluates 22 domains — every feature and quality that shapes what children can do in a space. From manufactured structures to loose natural materials, from sensory qualities to community context and management practices.

The insight behind this scope: Research shows that play spaces promote diverse play when they offer not only play equipment, but also open spaces, enclosed spaces, topographic features, natural features, loose parts, and malleable materials working in combination. How it works

Two methods. One structured instrument.

COPA combines an on-site audit of physical features with a site survey of operational context. They can be completed together or separately, depending on your access and familiarity with the playspace.

On-site audit
88 items

Conducted while walking the playspace. Evaluates observable physical characteristics. Completed by anyone familiar with outdoor play and environmental affordances.

EducatorsResearchersOTsLandscape architects
Site survey
36 items

Captures operational history and macro context. Completed by playspace managers, designers, or administrators familiar with the site's history and management practices.

ManagersAdministratorsDesigners
01
Select a playspace

Assign or select the playspace to be evaluated. Provide basic site information to anchor the audit.

02
Choose your evaluation mode

Complete the on-site audit, the site survey, or both — depending on your access, familiarity, and role.

03
Walk, observe, and record

For each domain, observe what the environment provides and rate it on the relevant scales. The app structures your observation and captures notes.

04
Generate scores and reports

Scores on Play Value, Usability, and all four scales are computed automatically. Reports identify highest- and lowest-scoring domains and support evidence-based planning.

Built for professionals who shape where children play.

Researchers

A methodologically grounded instrument with a clear citation trail, developed through systematic literature review and expert validation.

Occupational therapists

Evaluate and advocate for play environments that enable participation for children with diverse abilities — beyond compliance, toward genuine inclusion.

Park agencies & municipalities

Generate structured evidence to prioritize renovation investments, justify resource allocation, and document playspace quality across a portfolio of sites.

Research foundation

Grounded in evidence. Built with experts.

The COPA instrument — known in published research as the Playground Play Value and Usability Audit (PVUA) — was developed through a multi-stage, internationally collaborative process grounded in peer-reviewed research and validated with an interdisciplinary expert panel.

Systematic literature review

Developed from a scoping review of 6,095 references across seven academic databases — narrowed to 51 peer-reviewed studies on environmental qualities that enhance play for children with and without disabilities.

International expert panel validation

Content validity evaluated by 22 international, interdisciplinary experts who reviewed item relevance and clarity through iterative rounds of feedback and refinement.

Children's perspectives at the center

Grounded in research soliciting play experiences from children with and without disabilities — not proxy or adult-assumed preferences, but direct user evidence of what children need playgrounds to provide.

Affordance theory foundation

Built on Gibson's (1979) theory of affordances — shifting the evaluative frame from "what does this space contain?" to "what can a child potentially do here?" This supports a child-centered, occupation-focused analysis.
